Premium Financing for Life Insurance
in California

Legacy Planning Business Owners California

For high-net-worth California families, the policies best suited to long-term estate planning carry premiums that can reach hundreds of thousands of dollars annually. Premium financing offers a way to obtain that coverage without deploying significant liquid capital upfront — but it requires careful coordination and a clear-eyed understanding of both the benefits and the risks.

What Is Premium Financing for Life Insurance?

Premium financing is a strategy in which a third-party lender — typically a private bank or specialty lender — funds life insurance premiums on behalf of the insured. Rather than paying premiums out of pocket, the policyholder borrows the funds, with the life insurance policy and potentially other assets serving as collateral.

The strategy is most commonly used by:

Who Uses Premium Financing

  • High-net-worth individuals seeking large permanent life insurance policies for estate planning
  • Business owners protecting key person or buy-sell agreement coverage without disrupting operating capital
  • California families managing significant estate tax exposure who want coverage without liquidating assets
  • Individuals with high income but concentrated illiquid wealth — real estate, business interests, or private equity holdings

The core appeal is capital efficiency: obtain substantial life insurance coverage while keeping liquid capital deployed in higher-returning assets rather than paying premiums directly.

How Premium Financing Works in Practice

1

The insured applies for and is approved for a life insurance policy

2

A lending arrangement is established with a private bank or specialty lender

3

The lender pays the insurance premiums directly

4

The borrower provides collateral — typically the policy's cash value, plus additional assets if required

5

Interest accrues on the loan balance and is either paid annually or added to the loan

6

At a defined exit point — typically death, policy maturity, or a planned liquidity event — the loan is repaid from the policy's death benefit or cash value

When structured correctly and monitored carefully, premium financing can be a highly efficient estate planning tool. When poorly structured or inadequately monitored, it can create significant financial exposure.

California-Specific Considerations That Make Premium Financing More Complex

High Asset Concentrations

Many California high-net-worth families hold significant wealth in concentrated positions — real estate, technology equity, or private business interests. These assets may be used as collateral, but their valuation, liquidity, and marketability must be carefully assessed before pledging them.

Trust Structures and ILIT Coordination

Premium financing is frequently implemented inside an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust to keep death benefits outside the taxable estate. In California, coordinating premium financing with an ILIT requires careful attention to trust governance, Crummey notice requirements, and how loan obligations interact with trust administration.

Long-Term Tax Exposure

California's tax environment — including a 13.3% top marginal income tax rate and treatment of capital gains as ordinary income — makes the tax efficiency of premium financing particularly meaningful. However, the long-term tax implications of loan repayment, policy surrender, or lapse must be modeled carefully before implementation.

California Probate and Estate Administration

If a premium financed policy is part of a California estate plan, the interaction between loan obligations, collateral arrangements, and California probate should be addressed in advance — not at the time of death when options are limited.

The Risks of Premium Financing That Are Often Understated

Premium financing is a sophisticated strategy that carries real risks. Families considering this approach should understand them clearly before proceeding.

Risks to Understand Before Proceeding

  • Interest rate risk — loan interest rates are typically variable and can increase significantly over the life of the arrangement, affecting the economics of the strategy
  • Collateral call risk — if policy cash value underperforms or collateral values decline, lenders may require additional collateral or partial loan repayment
  • Policy performance risk — if the underlying life insurance policy does not perform as illustrated, the loan balance may exceed the policy's cash value over time
  • Lapse risk — a policy that lapses while a loan is outstanding can trigger significant taxable income, potentially creating a substantial unexpected tax liability
  • Complexity risk — premium financing involves multiple parties, documents, and ongoing obligations that require active management over many years

These risks are not reasons to avoid premium financing — they are reasons to approach it with conservative assumptions, rigorous modeling, and ongoing oversight.

What Proper Premium Financing Due Diligence Looks Like

Before implementing a premium financing strategy in California, a thorough evaluation should address:

Due Diligence Checklist

  • Liquidity and net worth assessment — is premium financing appropriate given the client's overall financial picture and risk tolerance?
  • Conservative interest rate modeling — what does the strategy look like if rates increase significantly from current levels?
  • Policy performance stress testing — how does the arrangement perform under conservative and pessimistic policy crediting assumptions?
  • Collateral analysis — what assets are being pledged, how are they valued, and what happens if their value declines?
  • Exit strategy planning — how and when will the loan be repaid, and what are the tax implications of each exit scenario?
  • Legal and tax coordination — have California estate planning attorneys and CPAs reviewed the structure?

Families who implement premium financing without addressing these questions thoroughly are taking on risk they may not fully understand.

Frequently Asked Questions: Premium Financing for Life Insurance in California

Who is premium financing for life insurance best suited for?

Premium financing is generally most appropriate for high-net-worth individuals with significant estate tax exposure, strong creditworthiness, and assets available for collateral — who want substantial life insurance coverage without deploying large amounts of liquid capital in premium payments. It is not appropriate for individuals with limited liquidity, high existing debt, or low risk tolerance.

What are the tax implications of premium financing in California?

When structured correctly, premium financing can be highly tax-efficient — keeping large death benefits outside the taxable estate while preserving liquid capital. However, poorly structured arrangements or policy lapses can create significant taxable income. California's high income tax rates make careful tax planning essential before implementation.

How are premium financing loans typically repaid in California?

Common repayment strategies include using the policy's death benefit at death, surrendering a portion of the policy's cash value at a planned exit point, or refinancing the loan using proceeds from a liquidity event such as a business sale or real estate transaction. The repayment strategy should be identified before the arrangement is implemented — not addressed reactively later.

What collateral is typically required for premium financing?

Lenders typically require the life insurance policy's cash value as primary collateral, with additional assets pledged if the cash value is insufficient. Common additional collateral includes real estate, investment accounts, or letters of credit. Collateral requirements vary by lender and are subject to annual review.

How does premium financing interact with an ILIT in California?

When premium financing is implemented inside an ILIT, the trust — not the insured — is the policy owner and borrower. This requires careful coordination between the trust document, lending agreement, and ongoing trust administration. California estate planning counsel should be involved in structuring and reviewing any ILIT premium financing arrangement.
