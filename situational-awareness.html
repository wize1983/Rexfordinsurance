Why Most Insurance Claims Were Preventable (And What to Do About It)

Risk Advisory Business Owners Rexford Insurance Solutions

You see it all the time. A security guard on his phone during school pickup. A wet floor that should have been cleaned hours ago. Security cameras installed — but not working. None of this is unusual. That's exactly the problem.

Small Risks Get Ignored —
Until They Become Claims

These aren't random incidents. They're patterns. Small issues that get overlooked, accepted, and repeated — until something goes wrong. That's how workplace accidents, liability claims, and costly losses happen. And the uncomfortable truth? Most of them were preventable.

👁
The security guard who leaves their post. Do you really have security? A slip and fall isn't bad luck — it's a condition that sat unaddressed for too long.
🔒
A break-in isn't always a hiring failure. It's an execution failure. A night guard who never checks the perimeter. A patrol log that says "all clear" when it wasn't.
📋
Sometimes the consequences are far more serious. A claim comes in after someone passes away. The incident report says security "checked the area." On paper, every protocol was followed. In reality, something critical was missed.

The Real Risk Isn't What's Missing —
It's What's There But Not Working

Where Claims Actually Come From

That gap — between what's supposed to happen and what actually happens — is where most claims and losses originate.

Insurance doesn't close that gap. Insurance doesn't stop the fall. It doesn't make the guard look up from his phone. It doesn't ensure the cameras are recording. It responds after the damage is done.

Walk into a retail store today and half the inventory is locked behind glass. Most of it got there after the loss. After the pattern. After somebody finally paid attention.

How Smart Operators Actually
Reduce Insurance Claims

The business owners and property managers who consistently reduce claims aren't just better insured — they're better operated. They treat risk management as an ongoing practice, not a checkbox.

  • Walk their properties regularly and look for what others overlook
  • Test their vendors and hold them accountable — not just at contract signing
  • Catch small issues before they become expensive problems
  • Review incident patterns, not just individual incidents
  • Treat "all clear" as something that needs to be earned, not assumed

Because most losses were preventable to begin with. Not with better coverage — with better attention.
